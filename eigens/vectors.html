Orthonogality

You might've heard that two lines can be "perpendicular." This is the term in two dimensions; in three or more, we call them "perpendicular."

› How can I determine if two vectors are orthogonal?

Cross product < x , y > describes the similarity of two vectors, x and y. It is calculated by adding up the element-wise products of the two vectors:


    x = [x1, x2, x3, ...]
    y = [y1, y2, y3, ...]
    < x , y > = x1×y1 + x2×y2 + x3×y3 + ...

The more similar the two vectors are, the greater the cross product. It is 0 if and only if they are orthogonal.

Examples:


    Standard vectors in 2D:
    x = [1, 0]
    y = [0, 1]
    <x,y> = 1×0 + 0×1 = 0
    -> orthogonal
                

    Weirder vectors in 2D:
    x = [1, 3]
    y = [-3, 1]
    <x,y> = 1×3 + -3×1 = 0
    -> orthogonal
